I need you to overhaul this real estate website to feel like a real person built it with intention — not like a vibe-coded template. The site currently works and has good bones, but every page looks the same: centered heading, gray subtitle, symmetrical 3-column card grid, CTA button. Repeat. It reads like one React component reused with different props across the whole site.
Here are the specific changes I want, organized by category:
1. LAYOUT — Break the Grid Monotony
Homepage hero: The current circle-headshot-left / text-right layout is the most generic agent site pattern possible. Replace it with something asymmetric. Try a full-width or near-full-width section where the photo is larger, more casual (not a circle crop), and the text has more breathing room. Consider overlapping elements or an off-center composition. Look at Compass agent pages for reference — they use generous whitespace with one strong focal point.
Kill the 3-column pattern: The "Why Work With Muzamil" section (3 icon cards), the "Who I Work With" section (3 service cards), and the sell page's 6-step process grid all use the same symmetrical card layout. Mix it up:

Make "Why Work With Muzamil" a staggered 2-column layout or a single scrolling feature section where each value prop gets real space
Turn the selling process steps into a proper vertical timeline with alternating left/right content, not a 2x3 grid
The "Who I Work With" cards should vary in size — make the "Primary Focus: First-Time Buyers" card genuinely larger/more prominent, not just labeled "Primary Focus" in the same-sized box

Neighborhoods page: The 4-card + 8-card grid is a wall of identical rectangles. Give the core neighborhoods (El Cerrito, Albany, Berkeley, Kensington) distinct visual treatment — maybe a larger card with a neighborhood photo, a key stat (median price, school rating, BART access), and a short personal take. The secondary neighborhoods can stay as a simpler list.
Section spacing: Every section has identical vertical padding. Vary it. Some sections should feel airy, others tighter. Add occasional full-bleed elements or subtle background shifts to create rhythm.
2. COPY — Sound Like a Person, Not a Brochure
Homepage intro paragraph: Replace "Your trusted El Cerrito, Albany & Berkeley Realtor®, backed by 15 years in the construction industry working with builders, developers, and investors. Get expert guidance for buying, selling, or relocating in the East Bay." This is generic agent copy. Rewrite it in first person, casual but confident. Something that sounds like Muzamil actually talking. Mention a specific detail — a neighborhood quirk, a recent market observation, something concrete. No buzzwords.
About page: The bio currently reads like a LinkedIn summary. Rewrite it conversationally. Include at least one personal detail that has nothing to do with real estate (what he does on weekends, favorite El Cerrito spot, why he moved there). Cut the corporate-sounding four value prop cards ("Honest, clear communication" / "Expert home valuation" / etc.) — these are table stakes that every agent claims. Replace them with 2-3 short, specific stories or examples that demonstrate these qualities instead of just stating them.
Repetition problem: The "15 years in construction" line appears in almost identical phrasing on the homepage, about page, buy page, and sell page. Write it differently each time, or better — show it instead of saying it. On the sell page, instead of claiming construction expertise, describe a specific scenario: "When a buyer's inspection comes back flagging foundation cracks, most agents panic. I've spent 15 years looking at foundations. I know which cracks are cosmetic and which ones matter."
Sell page: The "Usually Worth It / Often Not Worth It" prep section is good content but reads like a checklist from a blog post. Frame it as personal advice: "Here's what I tell every seller..." Make it feel like you're getting insider knowledge, not reading an article.
Blog posts: The titles sound like SEO articles ("How Mortgage Pre-Approval Actually Works"). If these are meant to build trust, they need a personal angle. Even small rewrites help: "What I Tell Buyers About Pre-Approval" or "The Pre-Approval Step Most Buyers Rush Through."
CTAs: "Book a free consult" appears everywhere in identical form. Vary the language: "Let's talk about your place," "Get my take on your neighborhood," "Ask me anything — seriously." Different pages should have CTAs that match the context.
3. TRUST & PROOF — Show, Don't Claim
Add real testimonials: Even 2-3 short ones with first names and neighborhoods. "Muzamil caught a drainage issue our inspector missed. Saved us $15K." — Sarah, El Cerrito. Place them inline within relevant sections, not in a separate testimonials carousel.
"5-star rated" needs proof: Link to the actual Zillow reviews or embed a review count. An unlinked star rating looks manufactured.
Add a "Recent Sales" or "Homes I've Helped With" section: Even without MLS data, a few cards with neighborhood + outcome ("Helped a first-time buyer close $30K under asking in Albany") would add massive credibility.
Neighborhood pages need local photography: Even stock photos of actual East Bay landmarks (El Cerrito Plaza, Solano Avenue, the Albany Bulb, Kensington hilltop views) would be better than text-only cards. These are visual neighborhoods — show them.
4. VISUAL & TYPOGRAPHY
Dark mode reconsideration: The all-dark-navy color scheme is unusual for real estate and feels more like a SaaS product. Consider keeping dark mode as an option but making the default a light, warm palette — off-white backgrounds, warm grays, with the navy as an accent. Real estate is about homes and warmth. The current dark scheme creates emotional distance.
Typography hierarchy: Every section heading is the same size and weight. Create more contrast — some headings should be noticeably larger (hero), some smaller and more intimate (section intros). Add a subtle serif font for headings to warm things up while keeping sans-serif for body text.
Add photography/imagery: The site is almost entirely text + icons. Real estate is visual. Add at least: a hero background image (East Bay hills, a neighborhood street), neighborhood photos on the neighborhoods page, and a more natural (less corporate) headshot or candid photo on the about page.
5. MOBILE PRIORITIES

Hero section should feel designed for mobile-first, not just "responsive"
Cards that are 3-across on desktop shouldn't just stack vertically on mobile — reconsider the mobile layout entirely
Contact info (phone, email) should be tap-to-call/tap-to-email and prominent on mobile
The nav should feel lightweight on mobile, not like a full desktop nav crammed into a hamburger

Implementation Notes

Keep it minimal and clean — the goal is "intentional and personal," not "busy"
